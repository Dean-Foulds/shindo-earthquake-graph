JAPANESE EARTHQUAKE EVENT REPORTS
Compiled for seismic risk analysis. Sources: JMA, Cabinet Office of Japan, IAEA, USGS.

================================================================================
EVENT 1: 2011 TOHOKU EARTHQUAKE AND TSUNAMI (東北地方太平洋沖地震)
================================================================================

On 11 March 2011 at 14:46 JST (05:46 UTC), a catastrophic earthquake occurred off the
Pacific coast of the Tohoku region of Japan. The earthquake had a moment magnitude of 9.1
and a hypocentral depth of approximately 29 kilometres. The epicentre was located at
latitude 38.297 degrees north and longitude 142.373 degrees east, within the Japan Trench
subduction zone approximately 70 kilometres east of the Oshika Peninsula, Miyagi Prefecture.
The fault type was subduction. The sea floor depth at the epicentre was approximately
minus 1,565 metres below sea level. JMA recorded a seismic intensity of 7 in parts of
Miyagi Prefecture, making it the strongest shaking level on the Japanese intensity scale.

Affected Prefectures and Cities

Miyagi Prefecture (JIS code 04) is a coastal prefecture on the Pacific coast of the
Tohoku region. It was the most severely affected prefecture. The city of Sendai, located
approximately 97 kilometres southwest of the epicentre, experienced intense shaking and
coastal inundation. The town of Minamisanriku, approximately 78 kilometres from the
epicentre, was almost entirely destroyed by the subsequent tsunami. The city of Ishinomaki,
approximately 80 kilometres from the epicentre, suffered catastrophic tsunami inundation.

Iwate Prefecture (JIS code 03) is a coastal prefecture north of Miyagi. The city of
Kamaishi, approximately 110 kilometres from the epicentre, was severely flooded. The town
of Rikuzentakata, approximately 125 kilometres from the epicentre, was almost completely
destroyed. Miyako city, approximately 145 kilometres north of the epicentre, also
experienced significant tsunami inundation.

Fukushima Prefecture (JIS code 07) is a coastal prefecture south of Miyagi. Coastal
communities including Iwaki and Soma experienced tsunami damage. The Futaba District,
approximately 180 kilometres from the epicentre, was severely affected by both the tsunami
and the subsequent nuclear incident at the Fukushima Daiichi power station.

Ibaraki Prefecture (JIS code 08) and Chiba Prefecture (JIS code 12) also experienced
damaging tsunami waves. Tokyo Prefecture (JIS code 13) and Kanagawa Prefecture (JIS code 14)
recorded lower shaking intensity but some coastal impacts.

Tsunami Warning and Event

The Japan Meteorological Agency issued a major tsunami warning (大津波警報) at 14:49 JST,
just three minutes after the earthquake occurred. This is consistent with JMA's target
response time of under three minutes. The warning was the highest level, indicating
expected wave heights greater than three metres.

The earthquake triggered a large-scale tsunami. Tsunami generation was confirmed across
the entire Pacific coast of Tohoku. The tsunami reached the nearest shore in approximately
ten minutes after generation. Travel time to the coast of Miyagi Prefecture was estimated
at between ten and fifteen minutes.

The wave profile was extreme. The wave height at the source was estimated at between
three and five metres. However, topographic amplification along the ria coastline of
Iwate and Miyagi dramatically increased wave heights at shore. The maximum wave runup
height recorded at shore was approximately 40.1 metres at Miyako, Iwate Prefecture.
Wave heights of 15 to 20 metres were recorded across wide sections of the Sanriku coast.
The average wave height at shore along the worst-affected coastline exceeded 10 metres.
Wave propagation speed across the open ocean was approximately 700 kilometres per hour.
The average distance from the epicentre to the nearest shore was approximately 130 kilometres.

Inundation Zones

In Miyagi Prefecture, the tsunami inundated an area of approximately 327 square kilometres.
Inundation distances inland reached up to 5 kilometres in low-lying coastal plains near
Sendai. The maximum inland elevation reached by the tsunami in Miyagi was approximately
12 metres above sea level. The cities of Sendai, Ishinomaki, and Natori were among the
most extensively inundated.

In Iwate Prefecture, the tsunami inundated approximately 58 square kilometres. The
inundation distance inland varied by location, reaching up to 3 kilometres in some valleys.
Maximum elevation reached was approximately 40 metres in Miyako. Kamaishi, Ofunato, and
Rikuzentakata were inundated.

In Fukushima Prefecture, the tsunami inundated approximately 112 square kilometres.
Coastal cities including Soma and Iwaki experienced tsunami flooding. Inundation reached
up to 3 kilometres inland in some coastal lowlands.

Tsunami Damage

The total number of fatalities attributed to the tsunami is estimated at approximately
19,500 persons. The number of persons reported missing after the tsunami was approximately
2,500. An estimated 129,225 buildings were washed away by the tsunami. An additional
254,204 buildings were reported flooded to varying degrees. These figures represent one
of the most destructive tsunami events in recorded Japanese history.

A formal tsunami damage report was published by the Cabinet Office of Japan on
11 April 2011, with subsequent revisions. The report was also contributed to by JMA
and the National Police Agency.

Shaking Damage

Damage caused directly by ground shaking, separate from the tsunami, included
approximately 130 fatalities. Injuries from shaking across the Tohoku and Kanto regions
numbered approximately 6,150. Buildings totally destroyed by shaking alone were
estimated at 11,000. An additional 144,000 buildings were classified as half destroyed.
Over 640,000 buildings reported partial damage from shaking.

A shaking damage report was compiled by the Cabinet Office on 12 March 2011.

Fire After Earthquake

Multiple fires broke out following the earthquake and tsunami. An estimated 330 fires
were recorded across affected prefectures, many ignited by gas line ruptures and
electrical faults caused by the shaking. In Kesennuma, Miyagi Prefecture, large fires
burned for several days, fed by oil from a fish processing facility ignited by the tsunami.
The estimated area burned in major post-earthquake fires totalled approximately 80 hectares
across the region. Buildings destroyed by fire after the earthquake numbered approximately
300. The fire in Kesennuma occurred in the coastal district of the city.

Landslide Risk

The earthquake triggered a critical landslide risk across much of the Tohoku interior.
Landslides did occur at multiple locations. The number of landslide events recorded
across the Tohoku region was approximately 150. The total volume of displaced material
was estimated in the tens of millions of cubic metres. Terrain types involved included
volcanic and sedimentary formations. Particular risk was noted in steep river valleys
in Iwate Prefecture.

Nuclear Incident

The Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Station, operated by Tokyo Electric Power Company
(TEPCO), is located in Okuma and Futaba towns, Fukushima Prefecture, approximately
180 kilometres south of the epicentre. The earthquake triggered an automatic SCRAM,
activating the emergency shutdown of the reactors. The SCRAM was activated successfully.
However, the tsunami that arrived approximately 51 minutes after the earthquake destroyed
the backup diesel generators, leading to a station blackout. The cooling systems were
subsequently lost. Units 1, 2, and 3 experienced core meltdowns. The event was rated INES
level 7, the maximum level on the International Nuclear Event Scale, equivalent in
classification to the 1986 Chernobyl disaster. The cooling system integrity was lost
within hours of the tsunami impact. Cooling was ultimately restored only after several
days of emergency intervention.

The Fukushima Daini Nuclear Power Station, also located in Fukushima Prefecture,
approximately 185 kilometres from the epicentre, experienced cooling difficulties but
achieved cold shutdown. Its INES level was rated 3. SCRAM was activated. Cooling systems
were ultimately maintained.

The Onagawa Nuclear Power Station, operated by Tohoku Electric Power Company, is located
in Onagawa town, Miyagi Prefecture, approximately 120 kilometres west of the epicentre.
Despite being closer to the epicentre than Fukushima Daiichi, the plant survived without
major damage due to a higher seawall. SCRAM was activated. Cooling systems remained intact.
The INES level was rated 0.

EVENT 2: 1995 GREAT HANSHIN EARTHQUAKE (阪神・淡路大震災)
================================================================================

On 17 January 1995 at 05:46 JST (20:46 UTC on 16 January), a major earthquake struck the
southern part of Hyogo Prefecture, Japan. The earthquake had a moment magnitude of 6.9 and
a hypocentral depth of approximately 16 kilometres. The epicentre was located at latitude
34.583 degrees north and longitude 135.035 degrees east, directly beneath Awaji Island.
The fault type was strike-slip, occurring on the Nojima Fault. The sea floor depth in the
vicinity of the epicentre was approximately minus 30 metres. JMA recorded a seismic
intensity of 7 in parts of Kobe and Ashiya, the first time in Japan's modern instrumented
history that intensity 7 was recorded.

Affected Prefectures and Cities

Hyogo Prefecture (JIS code 28) is a coastal prefecture on Osaka Bay. It was the most
severely affected prefecture. The city of Kobe, approximately 20 kilometres from the
epicentre, experienced catastrophic damage to its urban core. Ashiya city,
approximately 15 kilometres from the epicentre, also suffered severe structural damage.
The city of Nishinomiya, approximately 18 kilometres from the epicentre, and the city of
Takarazuka, approximately 22 kilometres from the epicentre, were heavily damaged.
Awaji Island, directly above the epicentre, experienced extreme shaking.

Osaka Prefecture (JIS code 27) is a coastal prefecture east of Hyogo. Parts of Osaka,
approximately 35 kilometres from the epicentre, experienced strong shaking and some
structural damage.

Tsunami Warning and Event

The earthquake did not generate a significant tsunami. No major tsunami warning was issued.
The shallow crustal nature of the fault rupture on a strike-slip mechanism did not produce
the vertical sea floor displacement required to generate a large tsunami. Tsunami generation
was not confirmed.

Shaking Damage

The Great Hanshin Earthquake caused catastrophic damage through direct ground shaking.
The total number of fatalities attributed to shaking and fire was 6,434. The number of
persons injured was approximately 43,792. Buildings totally destroyed by shaking numbered
approximately 104,906. Buildings classified as half destroyed totalled approximately 144,274.
Buildings partially damaged numbered over 390,000. Many of the destroyed buildings were
older wooden structures built before modern earthquake resistance standards were introduced.
The elevated Hanshin Expressway collapsed along a section of approximately 635 metres
in Higashinada Ward, Kobe. The Port of Kobe, a major international container terminal,
was severely damaged. A shaking damage report was published by the Cabinet Office of Japan
on 20 January 1995 and by the Hyogo Prefecture Government.

Fire After Earthquake

The Great Hanshin Earthquake triggered approximately 285 fires across the affected area,
the majority in Kobe. Many fires were caused by gas line ruptures and electrical faults.
In the Nagata Ward of Kobe, fires spread through densely packed wooden residential
districts and burned for over two days. The estimated area burned across Kobe totalled
approximately 83 hectares. Buildings destroyed by fire numbered approximately 7,000.
The fires in Nagata Ward, Kobe, were particularly severe, destroying entire residential
blocks. Gas line rupture was the primary cause identified for the majority of fires.
A fire damage report was published by the Kobe City Fire Department in February 1995.

Landslide Risk

The earthquake triggered numerous slope failures across Awaji Island and the hillside
residential areas of Kobe. The landslide risk level across steep terrain was assessed as
high to critical. Landslides did occur at multiple locations. Approximately 400 slope
failures were recorded across the affected region. Terrain types involved were primarily
weathered granite and volcanic formations common in the Rokko Mountain range. A landslide
risk assessment was compiled by the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism.

EVENT 3: 2024 NOTO PENINSULA EARTHQUAKE (令和6年能登半島地震)
================================================================================

On 1 January 2024 at 16:10 JST (07:10 UTC), a major earthquake struck the Noto Peninsula
of Ishikawa Prefecture, Japan. The earthquake had a moment magnitude of 7.6 and a
hypocentral depth of approximately 10 kilometres. The epicentre was located at latitude
37.486 degrees north and longitude 137.270 degrees east, on the Japan Sea side of Honshu.
The fault type was reverse, related to compressional tectonics beneath the Noto Peninsula.
The sea floor depth at the epicentre was approximately minus 200 metres. JMA recorded a
seismic intensity of 7 in parts of the Noto Peninsula, the first intensity-7 recording
in the Japan Sea coastal region.

Affected Prefectures and Cities

Ishikawa Prefecture (JIS code 17) is a coastal prefecture on the Japan Sea side of
central Honshu. It was the most severely affected prefecture. The city of Wajima,
approximately 45 kilometres from the epicentre, experienced catastrophic structural damage
and fire. The town of Suzu, approximately 55 kilometres from the epicentre, was severely
damaged with many buildings collapsed. The town of Anamizu, approximately 30 kilometres
from the epicentre, also suffered major damage. The city of Nanao, approximately 40
kilometres from the epicentre, was significantly affected.

Niigata Prefecture (JIS code 15) is a coastal prefecture northeast of Ishikawa. Coastal
communities in Niigata experienced minor tsunami waves and some shaking damage.

Toyama Prefecture (JIS code 16) is a coastal prefecture on the Japan Sea, bordering
Ishikawa. Some communities in Toyama experienced strong shaking.

Tsunami Warning and Event

The Japan Meteorological Agency issued a tsunami warning (津波警報) at 16:11 JST, within
one minute of the earthquake. For coastal areas of the Noto Peninsula and Toyama Bay,
a major tsunami warning (大津波警報) was issued. The warning indicated expected wave heights
of one to three metres along the Ishikawa coast and greater than three metres along the
Noto outer coast.

The earthquake triggered a tsunami. Tsunami generation was confirmed. The tsunami reached
the nearest shore of the Noto Peninsula in approximately five to seven minutes after
generation. This very short warning time presented a significant challenge for coastal
evacuation.

The wave height at the source was estimated at approximately one to two metres. Wave heights
at shore ranged from approximately 0.8 to 1.2 metres at tide gauges, though runup heights
at exposed coastal locations exceeded four metres in some areas. Wave speed across the
shallow Japan Sea was approximately 500 kilometres per hour. The average distance from the
epicentre to the outer Noto coastline was approximately 20 kilometres.

Inundation Zones

In Ishikawa Prefecture, the tsunami inundated approximately 10 square kilometres of coastal
lowland. Inundation distances inland reached up to 0.5 kilometres in the most exposed areas.
The maximum inland elevation reached was approximately 5 metres above sea level.
The town of Suzu and parts of Wajima coastline were inundated.

Tsunami Damage

The number of fatalities attributed to the tsunami was approximately 15 persons. Several
persons were reported missing in coastal areas. Buildings washed away by the tsunami
numbered approximately 100. Buildings flooded by the tsunami numbered several hundred.
A tsunami damage report was published by the Ishikawa Prefecture Government on 5 January 2024,
with subsequent updates. JMA also contributed to the report.

Shaking Damage

The earthquake caused extensive shaking damage across the Noto Peninsula. The number of
fatalities from shaking and building collapse was approximately 241. Injuries numbered
approximately 1,300. Buildings totally destroyed by shaking numbered approximately 8,000.
Buildings classified as half destroyed totalled approximately 20,000. Partial damage was
reported to over 100,000 buildings across Ishikawa Prefecture. Many structures destroyed
were traditional wooden townhouses in Wajima and Suzu. A shaking damage report was
published by the Cabinet Office of Japan on 3 January 2024.

Fire After Earthquake

Following the earthquake, a major fire broke out in the Asaichi-dori market district
of Wajima city. The fire, ignited by electrical faults and gas line ruptures from the
shaking, burned through a densely packed area of traditional wooden commercial buildings.
Approximately 7 fires were recorded across the Noto Peninsula in the immediate aftermath.
The Wajima market fire burned approximately 4.9 hectares and destroyed approximately 200
buildings. Electrical fault was identified as the primary cause of the Wajima fire.
The fire occurred in the Kawai-machi district of Wajima city. A fire damage report was
published by the Wajima City Fire Department in January 2024.

Landslide Risk

The earthquake triggered widespread slope failures across the Noto Peninsula, which is
characterised by steep terrain and weathered sedimentary and volcanic rock. Landslide risk
level across the peninsula was assessed as critical following the event. Landslides did
occur at many locations. Approximately 240 major slope failures were recorded on or near
the Noto Peninsula. The volume of displaced material from larger landslides was estimated
in the hundreds of thousands of cubic metres. Terrain types were predominantly sedimentary
with some volcanic formations. Several roads were blocked for weeks. A landslide risk
report was published by the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism in
January 2024.

EVENT 4: 2011 FUKUSHIMA AFTERSHOCK SEQUENCE (2011年4月)
================================================================================

On 11 April 2011 at 17:16 JST, a significant aftershock struck Fukushima Prefecture
following the March 9.1 mainshock. The earthquake had a moment magnitude of 7.1 and a
hypocentral depth of approximately 13 kilometres. The epicentre was located at latitude
36.945 degrees north and longitude 140.676 degrees east, inland in Fukushima Prefecture.
The fault type was normal. The sea floor depth at the epicentre was approximately positive
200 metres above sea level, as the epicentre was onshore. JMA recorded a seismic intensity
of 6 lower in parts of Fukushima Prefecture.

Affected Prefectures and Cities

Fukushima Prefecture (JIS code 07) was the most directly affected. The city of Iwaki,
approximately 20 kilometres from the aftershock epicentre, experienced significant shaking.
Tomioka town, approximately 30 kilometres from the epicentre, was also affected.
Ibaraki Prefecture (JIS code 08) experienced moderate shaking at sites approximately
60 kilometres from the epicentre.

Shaking Damage

This aftershock caused additional casualties and building damage in a region already
severely weakened by the March mainshock and tsunami. Shaking fatalities from this event
numbered approximately 3. Injuries were approximately 60. Buildings totally destroyed by
this aftershock numbered approximately 400. Buildings half destroyed numbered approximately
1,200. Partial damage was reported to thousands of additional structures across Fukushima
and Ibaraki. A shaking damage report was contributed to by the Cabinet Office and
Fukushima Prefecture Government.

Nuclear Incident

The Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Station, located approximately 38 kilometres north
of the aftershock epicentre, had already been in a severe accident state since March 11.
The aftershock did not trigger a new SCRAM as reactors were already shutdown. The cooling
system was not intact at this time, with units 1 through 3 experiencing ongoing meltdown
management. The INES level remained 7. This event is recorded as an additional seismic
challenge to an ongoing nuclear emergency.

EVENT 5: 2016 KUMAMOTO EARTHQUAKES (熊本地震)
================================================================================

A major earthquake sequence affected Kumamoto Prefecture, Kyushu, in April 2016. The
first significant event occurred on 14 April 2016 at 21:26 JST with a moment magnitude
of 6.2 and a hypocentral depth of approximately 11 kilometres. The epicentre was at
latitude 32.742 degrees north and longitude 130.809 degrees east near Mashiki town.
Fault type was strike-slip on the Futagawa Fault. JMA recorded intensity 7 at Mashiki.

Two days later, on 16 April 2016 at 01:25 JST, the main shock occurred with a moment
magnitude of 7.0 and a hypocentral depth of approximately 12 kilometres. The epicentre
was located at latitude 32.755 degrees north and longitude 130.757 degrees east.
Fault type was strike-slip. JMA recorded intensity 7 at Mashiki and Nishihara village,
the second intensity-7 recording within 28 hours at the same location.

Affected Prefectures and Cities

Kumamoto Prefecture (JIS code 43) was the most severely affected. Mashiki town,
approximately 5 kilometres from the mainshock epicentre, was almost entirely destroyed.
Minami-Aso village, approximately 20 kilometres southeast of the epicentre, was severely
damaged and experienced catastrophic landslides. The city of Kumamoto, approximately
15 kilometres from the epicentre, experienced significant damage particularly to the
historic Kumamoto Castle. Nishihara village, approximately 18 kilometres from the
epicentre, was also severely damaged.

Oita Prefecture (JIS code 44) experienced strong shaking and some structural damage,
approximately 50 kilometres from the mainshock epicentre.

Tsunami Warning and Event

No tsunami warning was issued. The inland, continental fault mechanism of the Kumamoto
earthquakes did not produce a tsunami. Tsunami generation was not confirmed.

Shaking Damage

The combined damage from the foreshock and mainshock sequence resulted in approximately
273 fatalities. Injuries numbered approximately 2,809. Buildings totally destroyed numbered
approximately 8,667. Buildings half destroyed totalled approximately 34,719. Partial damage
was reported to over 160,000 structures across Kumamoto Prefecture. Many historic wooden
structures and older buildings suffered collapse. A shaking damage report was published
by the Cabinet Office of Japan on 18 April 2016.

Fire After Earthquake

Approximately 15 fires were recorded across Kumamoto Prefecture following the earthquake
sequence. Gas line ruptures and electrical faults were the primary causes. The estimated
area burned was approximately 2 hectares. Buildings destroyed by fire numbered approximately
40. Several fires occurred in the city of Kumamoto.

Landslide Risk

The earthquake sequence triggered catastrophic landslides in the steep volcanic terrain
of the Aso Caldera region. Landslide risk was assessed as critical across the mountainous
zones of southern Kumamoto Prefecture. Landslides did occur at many locations.
Approximately 190 major slope failures were recorded. A massive landslide in Minami-Aso
village blocked National Route 57 and caused the collapse of the Aso Bridge. Volume of
displaced material was estimated in the millions of cubic metres. Terrain types were
primarily volcanic, with pyroclastic deposits common in the Aso region. A landslide risk
assessment was published by the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism
in May 2016.

EVENT 6: 2003 TOKACHI-OKI EARTHQUAKE (十勝沖地震)
================================================================================

On 26 September 2003 at 04:50 JST (19:50 UTC on 25 September), a major earthquake struck
off the coast of Tokachi, Hokkaido. The earthquake had a moment magnitude of 8.3 and a
hypocentral depth of approximately 27 kilometres. The epicentre was at latitude 41.779
degrees north and longitude 144.079 degrees east, within the Kuril-Kamchatka subduction
zone. Fault type was subduction. The sea floor depth at the epicentre was approximately
minus 2,100 metres below sea level. JMA recorded a seismic intensity of 6 upper in parts
of Tokachi and Kushiro.

Affected Prefectures and Cities

Hokkaido Prefecture (JIS code 01) was the most severely affected. The city of Obihiro,
approximately 130 kilometres from the epicentre, experienced strong shaking and
significant damage to older structures. Kushiro city, approximately 100 kilometres from
the epicentre, also experienced strong shaking. The town of Taiki, approximately 80
kilometres from the epicentre, was affected by both shaking and small tsunami waves.

Tsunami Warning and Event

JMA issued a tsunami warning (津波警報) within three minutes of the earthquake. The warning
indicated expected wave heights of one to two metres on the Pacific coast of Hokkaido.
The earthquake triggered a tsunami. Tsunami generation was confirmed. The tsunami reached
the coast of Tokachi in approximately 10 minutes after generation.

The wave height at the source was estimated at approximately 1.5 metres. Wave heights at
shore on the Tokachi coast ranged from approximately 1.0 to 2.0 metres. Wave speed was
approximately 700 kilometres per hour across the open Pacific Ocean. Distance from the
epicentre to the Tokachi coast was approximately 80 kilometres.

Inundation Zones

In Hokkaido, the tsunami inundated approximately 5 square kilometres of low-lying coastal
land near the Tokachi River mouth. Inundation distances inland reached up to 0.3 kilometres.
The maximum inland elevation reached was approximately 2 metres above sea level.
Taiki town coastline and the Tokachi River lowlands were inundated.

Tsunami Damage

No fatalities were directly attributed to the tsunami. Buildings washed away were
negligible. Approximately 50 buildings experienced flooding from the tsunami.
A tsunami damage report was published by the Hokkaido Prefecture Government in October 2003.

Shaking Damage

Shaking damage resulted in approximately 2 fatalities. Injuries numbered approximately 849.
Buildings totally destroyed by shaking numbered approximately 116. Buildings half destroyed
totalled approximately 368. Partial damage was reported to approximately 9,800 structures.
Agricultural facilities, storage tanks, and older buildings were the most affected. A
shaking damage report was published by the Cabinet Office in October 2003.

Landslide Risk

The earthquake triggered slope failures in the hilly terrain south and east of Obihiro.
Landslide risk was assessed as moderate across the Tokachi interior. Landslides did occur.
Approximately 20 slope failures were recorded. Terrain types were predominantly sedimentary
with glacial deposits. A landslide assessment was contributed to by the Ministry of Land,
Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism.
